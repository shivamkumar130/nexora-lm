Classification of Errors Errors are mainly classified into: Systematic Errors Random Errors 7. Systematic Errors Systematic errors are errors whose causes are known and can usually be corrected. These errors occur in the same direction repeatedly. Characteristics: Predictable Reproducible Can be reduced Types of Systematic Errors A. Instrumental Errors Errors caused by imperfections in measuring instruments. Examples: Defective scale Incorrect calibration Zero error B. Personal Errors Errors caused by the observer. 1. 2. Examples: Wrong reading Wrong recording Parallax error C. Environmental Errors Errors caused by surroundings. Examples: Temperature changes Humidity Pressure variation Magnetic fields 8. Random Errors Random errors occur due to unknown and unpredictable causes. Characteristics: Irregular Unpredictable Cannot be eliminated completely Examples: Human reaction time Small environmental fluctuations Reduction of Random Errors Random errors can be minimized by: Taking multiple observations. Finding the arithmetic mean. 1. 2. 9. Mean (True) Value Suppose a quantity is measured n times. Observations: a₁, a₂, a₃,...., aₙ The mean value is: aₘ = (a₁ + a₂ + a₃ +... + aₙ)/n This mean value is considered the most probable value of the measurement. Example Observations: 10 cm, 11 cm, 9 cm, 10 cm Mean value: aₘ = (10 + 11 + 9 + 10)/4 aₘ = 40/4 aₘ = 10 cm 10. Absolute Error The absolute error for each observation is the numerical difference between the mean value and the observed value. For first observation: Δa₁ = |aₘ − a₁| For second observation: Δa₂ = |aₘ − a₂| Similarly, Δaₙ = |aₘ − aₙ| Example Observations: 10 cm, 11 cm, 9 cm, 10 cm Mean value = 10 cm Absolute errors: |10 − 10| = 0 |10 − 11| = 1 |10 − 9| = 1
