Physical Quantities A physical quantity is any quantity that can be measured and expressed numerically along with a unit. Examples: Length Mass Time Velocity Force Temperature Physical quantities are classified into: Scalar Quantities Vector Quantities Tensor Quantities 2. Scalar Quantities Definition A scalar quantity is a physical quantity that possesses magnitude only. Direction is not required. Characteristics Has only magnitude. No direction. Added using ordinary algebra. Completely specified by a numerical value and unit. Examples Mass Length Distance Area Volume Density Work Energy Temperature Speed Example Mass = 10 kg 1. 2. 3. This statement is complete. Direction is not required. Hence mass is a scalar quantity. 3. Vector Quantities Definition A vector quantity is a physical quantity that possesses both magnitude and direction. Characteristics Has magnitude. Has direction. Follows vector laws. Cannot be added using ordinary algebra. Examples Displacement Velocity Acceleration Force Momentum Electric Field Magnetic Field Example Velocity = 20 m/s East Magnitude = 20 m/s Direction = East Therefore velocity is a vector. Difference Between Scalars and Vectors Scalar Vector Magnitude onlyMagnitude and direction Ordinary algebraVector algebra No direction Direction essential Example: Mass Example: Force 4. Tensor Quantities Definition Tensor quantities are quantities having magnitude and direction but following special mathematical laws beyond ordinary vector algebra. Examples Stress Strain Moment of Inertia Tensor Conductivity Tensor Tensor analysis is usually studied in advanced physics. 5. Representation of Vectors Vectors are represented by directed line segments. A vector is denoted by: A⃗ or AB⃗ Directed Line Segment Length of line → Magnitude Arrow head → Direction Example: A ----------→ B Vector: AB⃗ 6. Magnitude of a Vector Magnitude represents the size of the vector. If vector is: A⃗ its magnitude is written as: |A⃗| or Example If |A⃗| = 5 then magnitude of vector is 5 units. 7. Zero
