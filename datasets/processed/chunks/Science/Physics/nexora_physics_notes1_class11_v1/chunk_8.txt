Cannot Determine Numerical Constants Dimensional analysis cannot determine numerical constants such as: 1/2 Example: Area of circle A = πr² Using dimensions we can find that area is proportional to r² but cannot determine the value of π. 2. Cannot Distinguish Between Similar Quantities Many physical quantities possess the same dimensional formula. Examples: Work Dimension: [ML²T⁻²] Energy Dimension: [ML²T⁻²] Torque Dimension: [ML²T⁻²] All have the same dimensions but different physical meanings. 3. Cannot Derive Trigonometric Functions Dimensional analysis cannot establish equations involving: sinθ cosθ tanθ logarithms exponential functions Example: y = A sin(ωt) cannot be derived completely using dimensions. 4. Cannot Check Vector or Scalar Nature Dimensions only provide magnitude information. They do not indicate whether a quantity is: Scalar Vector Example: Work and Torque possess identical dimensions but differ in nature. COMMONLY USED PHYSICAL QUANTITIES Force Definition: Force is an external agency that changes or attempts to change the state of motion of a body. Formula: F = ma SI Unit: Newton (N) Dimension: [MLT⁻²] Work Definition: Work is said to be done when force causes displacement. Formula: W = Fs SI Unit: Joule (J) Dimension: [ML²T⁻²] Energy Definition: Capacity to perform work. SI Unit: Joule Dimension: [ML²T⁻²] Power Definition: Rate of doing work. Formula: P = W/t SI Unit: Watt Dimension: [ML²T⁻³] Pressure Definition: Force acting per unit area. Formula: P = F/A
