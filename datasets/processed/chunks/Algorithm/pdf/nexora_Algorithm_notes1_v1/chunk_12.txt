Once all edges of \(v \) are explored, DFS **backtracks** to the vertex from which \(v I) was discovered. This process continues until all vertices reachable from the initial source vertex are discovered. If there are any **undiscovered vertices** remaining in the graph, DFS selects one of them as a new source and starts a new search. This repeats until all vertices in the graph have been discovered. # **Key Features of DFS** 1. **Predecessor Subgraph and Forest:**. - When DFS discovers a vertex \(v \) while scanning the adjacency list of another vertex \(u 1), it records \(u \) as the **predecessor** of \(v \) by setting \(v.\pi = u I). - Unlike BFS, which produces a single tree as the predecessor subgraph, DFS may create multiple trees, forming a **depth-first forest**. This is because DFS may restart from multiple sources if the graph has disconnected components.. The predecessor subgraph is defined as:. G_\pi = (V, E_\pi) where: E_\pi = \{ (v\pi, v) \mid v \in V\text{and} v.\pi \neq\text{NIL} \} The edges in \(E_\pi I) are referred to as **tree edges**.. 2. **Vertex Coloring:** - DFS uses a coloring mechanism to track the state of each vertex during the search:. - **WHITE**: Vertex is undiscovered. - **GRAY**: Vertex is discovered but not fully explored.. - This ensures that each vertex belongs to exactly one depth-first tree, and the trees are. disjoint. 3. **Timestamps:** - DFS assigns two timestamps to each vertex \(v \): - \(v.d \): **Discovery time** - the time when \(v \) is first discovered (colored GRAY). - \(v.f \): **Finishing time** - the time when \(v \) is fully explored (colored BLACK). - Timestamps are integers between 1 and \(2IVI \), since there are two events (discovery and finishing) for each
