Q3 - Counting semaphore / one erroneous process. Problem setup (interpreted): Ten processes share a critical section using a counting semaphore x initially 1. Nine processes correctly do:. wait(x); CriticalSection; signal(x); One process erroneously does: signal(x); CriticalSection; signal(x); Question: What is the maximum number of processes that can be inside the critical section at the same time? Answer: 3 processes (maximum) Why (short): Start x = 1. The erroneous process does signa1(x) before entering -> x becomes 2 Then that erroneous process enters its critical section (without waiting). Meanwhile up to two of the correct processes can perform wait(x) while x is positive, decrementing it and entering the critical section. That gives at most: the erroneous process + two correct processes = 3 concurrently inside. The erroneous process's later signa1 happens after it leaves and cannot increase the simultaneous count at the same instant. Q4 - When is Rate-Monotonic (RM) inferior to Earliest-Deadline-First (EDF)? Answer (concise): RM is inferior when task sets have utilization or deadline patterns that EDF can schedule but RM cannot. Concretely: RM is static priority and has a schedulability bound: for n periodic tasks, guaranteed schedulability if total utilization U n(2^{1/n} - 1) (~ 69% as n ->). EDF (dynamic priority) can schedule many task sets with U up to 100% (in theory) that RM cannot. Also when deadlines are dynamic or deadlines periods or tasks are not strictly periodic, EDF's dynamic priorities give it an advantage. patterns where EDF can meet deadlines and RM cannot), EDF will succeed where RM fails. Q5 - Can a system detect starvation? If yes how? If no, how to deal with starvation? Answer: Yes - detection and mitigation both possible. Detection: The system can monitor per-process waiting times (time spent ready but not scheduled)
